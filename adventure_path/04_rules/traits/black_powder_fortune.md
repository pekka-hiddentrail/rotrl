# Black Powder Fortune

**Type:** Combat Trait  
**Source:** Ultimate Campaign, p.51  
**Characters who have it:** *(update when assigned to a character)*  
**Prerequisites:** None

---

## Description

> You have little to fear when there's a gun in your hand. As long as you are
> wielding a firearm, you gain a +2 trait bonus on all saving throws against
> curse, fear, and emotion effects.

---

## Mechanical Advantage

- **+2 trait bonus on saves vs. curse, fear, and emotion effects** — but only
  while actively wielding a firearm. If the gun is holstered, sheathed, or not
  in hand, the bonus is inactive.
- **Covers three save categories simultaneously:** curses (bestow curse, geas),
  fear effects (cause fear, frightful presence, shaken/frightened/panicked
  conditions), and emotion effects (rage, crushing despair, hideous laughter).
- **+2 trait is notably large** — most combat traits provide +1. This is one of
  the stronger single-source trait bonuses available.
- **Weapon in hand is required.** Drawing a firearm is a standard action (or
  move action with Quick Draw), so the bonus may not be available in a surprise
  round if the gun was stowed.
- **Applies to all three save types** (Fortitude, Reflex, Will) for qualifying
  effects — saving throw type is determined by the individual spell or ability,
  not by this trait.

### In Practice

Fear effects are among the most disabling conditions in PF1e — a frightened
character must flee; a panicked character drops everything. +2 vs. fear while
armed with a gun is a permanent floor on morale resilience for a firearm-
focused character. Against a Goblin Warchanter's Inspiring Song or any undead
fear aura, this trait is the difference between standing firm and routing.

### Firearm Wield Requirement

The firearm must be wielded, not merely carried. In combat this generally means
it is in the character's hand (grip, not holster). Reloading counts as wielding.
If the character is disarmed, the bonus immediately lapses until the firearm is
recovered and re-gripped.
