# Aldern Foxglove

**Aliases:** aldern, foxglove, aldern foxglove, noble
**Locations:** rusty dragon, inn, festival grounds, festival square

## Personality

Charming, generous, and quietly desperate for connection. Aldern presents himself as a well-travelled gentleman at ease with danger — and during Act I, this is mostly true. Beneath the surface he is already unstable, though this is not visible yet. He attaches quickly and intensely to people who impress him. His gratitude can feel overwhelming if you pay attention.

## Location & Availability — Act I

- **Festival day:** Present as a social attendee; likely near the food and entertainment
- **During goblin attack:** May approach PCs who act heroically; grateful and visibly shaken
- **After the attack:** Returns to his lodgings; may seek out PCs who helped him
- **General:** Sandpoint visitor; not a permanent resident; will leave after a few days

## Reaction to PCs

Openly grateful to anyone who protected him or others during the attack. Generous — he offers to fund a hunting trip or share a meal. Becomes attentive and slightly over-eager if PCs engage warmly. If rebuffed, he masks his disappointment well and withdraws. Does not push.

## Social Checks

- **Diplomacy:** DC 6; he wants to be liked and is easy to warm up to
- **Sense Motive:** DC 18 to detect the undercurrent of instability behind his charm
- **Bluff:** DC 12 if trying to discourage his attention without offending him

## State Handling

- **If Befriended:** Obsession forms; becomes personal and increasingly dangerous in Book II
- **If Ignored:** Obsession intensifies privately; no visible change during Act I
- **If Rebuffed Harshly:** Resentment accelerates his descent; accelerates Book II timeline
- **If Killed in Act I:** Book II requires significant restructuring around his absence

**Enforcement:** Aldern must never demand attention or seem important. His significance is only visible in hindsight. Play him as pleasant background.

## Narrative Function

Seeds the horror of Book II through mundane, pleasant interaction. His importance in Act I is entirely social. The GM must never make him seem threatening or significant — his danger only becomes apparent in hindsight. He is charming because the story needs the players to like him before it uses him.
